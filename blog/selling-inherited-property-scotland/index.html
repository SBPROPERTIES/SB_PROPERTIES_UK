Selling an Inherited Property in Scotland (2026 Guide)

Written by SB Properties UK Expert Team • Last Updated: 2026

Losing a loved one is incredibly difficult, and dealing with their property can feel overwhelming. In Scotland, the legal process for handling an estate is unique and different from England or Wales.

Whether you've inherited a tenement flat in Glasgow or a family home in Paisley, this 2026 guide explains exactly how to navigate the "Confirmation" process and your options for a fast, stress-free sale.

What is Confirmation in Scotland?

In Scotland, "Confirmation" is the legal document issued by the Sheriff Court that gives the executors authority to settle the estate and sell assets—including property. It is the Scottish equivalent of "Probate."

1. The Initial Valuation

Before applying for Confirmation, you must value the entire estate. This includes the property's market value at the time of death. Even if you plan on getting a 24 Hour Cash Offer, you will still need a formal valuation for the court forms.

2. Submitting Forms to the Sheriff Court

Your solicitor will submit form C1 (the Inventory of the estate). If Inheritance Tax is due, additional HMRC forms (IHT400) are required. In 2026, the Sheriff Courts are processing these at varied speeds depending on the region.

Can you sell the house before Confirmation is granted?

You can market the property and even accept an offer, but you cannot legally "settle" (transfer the title and receive funds) until the Confirmation document is in your hands. This is why many executors choose a cash buyer—we can wait for the legal paperwork to clear while providing you with a guaranteed price upfront.

Common Roadblocks for Inherited Sales

Selling an inherited home often comes with challenges that traditional estate agents struggle to manage:

  • The "Clean-Out" Burden: Decades of belongings can take months to clear.
  • Condition Issues: Many inherited properties need significant modernization (new boilers, wiring, or damp treatment).
  • The Home Report Cost: In Scotland, the seller must pay upfront for a Home Report. If the house is in poor repair, the Home Report may highlight issues that scare off mortgage buyers.
  • Sibling Disputes: If multiple beneficiaries are involved, a long, drawn-out sale can create tension.
